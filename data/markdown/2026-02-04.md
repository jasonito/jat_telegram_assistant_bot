Jensen framed a benchmark: about $50B of capex for a 1GW datacenter. That number belongs in the same mental shelf as iconic public works. The Golden Gate Bridge cost roughly $35M in the 1930s and opened in 1937. It still carries daily life nearly a century later. Both types of projects are bets on flow. One moves people and goods across water. One moves computation across networks. Both require power systems, supply chains, maintenance cultures, and rules that decide who benefits and who pays. A useful lens is simple: cost, use life, and spillover. The sticker price is visible. The real story is how long the asset stays useful, and how widely its gains diffuse into society.

A bridge has a public presence. It ages in full view. It becomes part of a cityâ€™s identity and a regionâ€™s productivity. Its benefits are easy to narrate: time saved, trade expanded, labor markets widened, neighborhoods connected, resilience improved. A datacenter is a quieter monument. Concrete, transformers, cooling, fiber, and racks that turn electricity into capability. The building can last decades. The hardware refreshes quickly. The output is less tangible, and it still reshapes welfare. Faster research cycles. Cheaper experimentation. New tools in medicine, education, manufacturing, and design. New dependencies in security, energy, and governance. When capability becomes infrastructure, the biggest variable is stewardship.

If a $50B, 1GW datacenter is treated as modern infrastructure, what civic expectations travel with it? Reliability, safety, accountability, and resilience have always been part of the bridge bargain. Compute raises parallel questions: access, transparency, competitive openness, energy discipline, and who captures the spillovers. The Golden Gate Bridge created a shared shortcut through geography. Compute creates shortcuts through complexity. Both change the map. Both concentrate power unless deliberate choices distribute it. The comparison is not a verdict. It is an invitation. Which asset feels more like a public work, and what return should society expect when the next monument is mostly invisible?
